# Deploying Geospatial Intelligence in Building Nigeria's Next Marketplace

**Published:** March 21, 2026
**Author:** Alex Nwoko
**Tag:** Founder Perspective
**Read Time:** 8 min read

---

## Most marketplace founders come from business or engineering. I came from maps.

My background is in geospatial technology — GIS, remote sensing, spatial analytics. Before I ever thought about building a consumer marketplace, I was thinking about how location shapes human behaviour. Where people go. How resources cluster. Why distance matters more than most technologists admit.

That background turns out to be the single most important thing about how Vendoh was designed.

## Seeing the world through a spatial lens

When most people look at Lagos, they see chaos — 20 million people moving through a city that wasn't built for that kind of density. When I look at Lagos through a geospatial lens, I see patterns. I see service economies that cluster around transport nodes. I see informal markets that form and dissolve based on foot traffic patterns. I see that a 5km radius in Lekki contains an entirely different service economy than a 5km radius in Surulere, even though both are "Lagos."

This spatial perspective is what led me to the core insight behind Vendoh's earliest ancestor, Food Vendor Naija, which I first wrote about on a blog I literally called **Geospatial Innovation**. The idea wasn't just "let's build a food app." It was "let's use location intelligence to make invisible economic activity visible."

In Nigeria, there are millions of skilled service providers — caterers, tailors, electricians, makeup artists, photographers — operating within hyperlocal neighbourhoods. Their customers live within walking distance. But without a digital discovery layer, both sides are blind. A plumber in your own estate might be excellent, affordable, and available right now, but you'd never know it. You'd be scrolling through WhatsApp broadcasts or asking friends instead.

GIS taught me that **proximity is the most undervalued variable in marketplace design**. Most global marketplaces treat location as a filter. In Vendoh, it's the foundation.

## How spatial thinking shaped Vendoh's architecture

The 5km radius proximity engine at the heart of Vendoh isn't a borrowed feature from Western delivery apps. It emerged from analysing how service transactions actually happen in Nigerian cities.

In my geospatial work, I studied how economic activity distributes across urban areas. What I consistently found was that most informal service transactions happen within remarkably small geographic zones. The tailor your family uses is in your neighbourhood. The event caterer you trust was recommended by someone on your street. The electrician who shows up quickly lives nearby. This isn't accidental; it's rational. In a city with unpredictable traffic, proximity equals reliability.

Vendoh's architecture respects this reality. When you open the app, your marketplace is your immediate neighbourhood. Every vendor you see, every service provider you're matched with, is within a distance that makes physical service delivery practical. This isn't a limitation — it's a design principle informed by spatial analysis of how Nigerian commerce naturally operates.

## From remote sensing to voice search

There's a less obvious connection between my geospatial background and Vendoh's voice-first interface, but it's real.

In remote sensing, you learn to extract meaningful information from noisy, unstructured data. A satellite image is millions of pixels that mean nothing until you process and interpret them. The skill is turning raw signal into actionable insight.

Voice search in a marketplace context is the same challenge at a different scale. When someone says "I need someone to do my makeup for an owambe next Saturday in Ikeja," that's unstructured data — natural language with geographic references, temporal constraints, service categories, and cultural context all embedded in a single sentence. Vendoh's AI needs to parse all of that, just as a remote sensing algorithm parses spectral bands to identify land use patterns.

My training in geospatial analysis — where every data point has a spatial and temporal dimension — directly informed how I think about processing service requests. Location isn't just "where." It's context. It tells you about urgency, availability, pricing, and cultural expectations. A request for catering in Victoria Island two days before December carries entirely different implications than the same request in Festac in March.

## Why this perspective matters for African tech

Africa's tech ecosystem has largely followed the playbook of Silicon Valley and Y Combinator: take a proven Western model, adapt it for local context, and scale. That approach has produced some remarkable companies, but it has a blind spot. It starts with the technology and works backwards to the market.

Coming from GIS, I started from the opposite direction. I started with the territory — the physical, spatial, economic reality of how Nigerian cities function — and worked forward to the technology that could serve it. The difference is subtle but consequential.

A technology-first marketplace asks: "How do we get users onto our platform?" A spatially-informed marketplace asks: "Where are the existing economic relationships, and how do we make them more efficient?" Vendoh doesn't create a market. It reveals the one that already exists and gives it digital infrastructure.

This is why Vendoh isn't trying to be the Uber or TaskRabbit of Nigeria. Those analogies miss the point. Vendoh is a geospatially-aware system designed from first principles for how African informal economies actually work — hyperlocal, relationship-driven, trust-dependent, and voice-native.

## The map that led here

When I published that Food Vendor Naija concept on my Geospatial Innovation blog in 2019, I was trying to solve what I saw as fundamentally a **spatial problem**: valuable economic activity that was invisible because it lacked geographic representation in digital systems. Seven years later, Vendoh is the most sophisticated answer I can build to that problem.

Every technical decision — the proximity engine, the voice processing, the vendor matching algorithms — traces back to a simple question that GIS taught me to ask: **Where are things, and why does that matter?**

It turns out, for Nigeria's 40-million-strong informal service economy, it matters more than almost anything else.
