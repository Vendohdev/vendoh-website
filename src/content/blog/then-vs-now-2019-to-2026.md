# Then vs. Now: How a 2019 Food Delivery App Became Nigeria's Voice-Powered Service Marketplace

**Published:** March 21, 2026
**Author:** Alex Nwoko
**Tag:** Origin Story
**Read Time:** 7 min read

---

## A tale of two products

In August 2019, I published a blog post on my Geospatial Innovation website describing a mobile app called **Food Vendor Naija**. Around the same time, I recorded a short promotional video walking people through the concept — download, register, pick your role, start ordering. It was scrappy, it was early, and it was the beginning of something I couldn't fully see yet.

Seven years later, that idea has become **Vendoh** — a voice-powered marketplace covering 321+ service categories across Nigeria's informal economy. The journey between those two points is a story about what changes when a founder listens, and what stays the same when the core insight is right.

## What Food Vendor Naija looked like in 2019

The original concept was focused entirely on food. The problem was clear: busy Nigerians needed a reliable way to find food vendors near them and get meals delivered quickly. The solution was a mobile app — available on both Android and iOS — that connected three types of users: customers ordering food, vendors selling food, and delivery agents moving food from point A to point B.

The architecture was more sophisticated than a typical food delivery app, though. I had mapped out hygiene verification systems through partnerships with community health offices. I designed a tiered membership model where vendors could earn Platinum status through consistent five-star reviews. I planned for real-time tracking, location-based discovery, and a growth strategy that targeted 30 Nigerian cities by 2020.

The onboarding was intentionally simple — a three-step process designed to get anyone from download to first order in minutes. Even then, I understood that in Nigeria's market, accessibility and simplicity weren't nice-to-haves; they were everything.

## What Vendoh looks like in 2026

Fast-forward to today, and every dimension of the original concept has expanded.

**Scope:** Food Vendor Naija served food vendors. Vendoh serves 321+ categories — from event planners and makeup artists to electricians and fashion designers. The insight that drove the expansion was obvious once I saw it: the discovery and trust problems affecting food vendors were identical across Nigeria's entire informal service economy.

**Interface:** Food Vendor Naija had a standard mobile app interface. Vendoh is voice-first. Users speak their needs naturally — "I need a caterer for 50 people in Lekki this Saturday" — and AI processes the request, matches providers, and starts the booking flow. This isn't a gimmick; it reflects how Nigerians actually communicate. Voice notes dominate messaging apps here, and Vendoh is built for that reality.

**Discovery:** The original concept had location-based features, but Vendoh takes this to another level with a 5km radius proximity engine. Every user's experience is hyperlocal by default. Your neighbourhood is your marketplace, with the option to expand when you need to.

**Trust:** Food Vendor Naija had hygiene verification and review systems. Vendoh has a comprehensive vendor verification framework, accountability mechanisms, and a ratings system designed to reward consistency over time. The principle is the same — trust is the currency of informal commerce — but the execution is far more robust.

**Technology:** In 2019, I was thinking in terms of mobile apps, GPS tracking, and database management. In 2026, Vendoh runs on AI models, natural language processing, and intelligent matching algorithms. The technology stack has transformed, but it serves the same purpose: connecting people who need services with people who provide them.

## What stayed the same

Here's what's remarkable when I look at these two versions side by side: the DNA hasn't changed.

The three-sided marketplace model — customer, provider, and facilitator — is still the structural foundation. The conviction that trust and verification must be baked into the platform, not bolted on afterwards, remains unchanged. The focus on Nigeria's informal economy as the primary market, with all its complexity and potential, has never wavered. And the belief that technology should make existing economic relationships more visible and more reliable — rather than replacing them with something foreign — continues to drive every product decision.

The evolution from Food Vendor Naija to Vendoh isn't a pivot story. It's a deepening story. The seed was right; it just needed more soil.

## What this means for what we're building

I share this history because I believe it matters for how people understand Vendoh. This isn't a marketplace that was dreamed up in a weekend hackathon or inspired by copying a Western model. It was born from years of observing how Nigerian commerce actually functions — the WhatsApp groups, the word-of-mouth networks, the trust built through personal relationships — and asking how technology could support and scale those existing patterns rather than replacing them.

When we launch, every feature you interact with carries the weight of that seven-year journey. The voice search exists because I watched Nigerians communicate. The proximity matching exists because I studied spatial patterns. The verification system exists because I understood that trust, not technology, is the real infrastructure gap.

From Food Vendor Naija to Vendoh. From 2019 to 2026. The journey continues.
