# Building Vendoh: What It's Really Like to Build a Tech Startup Solo in Lagos

**Published:** March 14, 2026
**Author:** Alex Nwoko
**Tag:** Founder Journey
**Read Time:** 8 min read

---

There is a genre of startup writing that I do not want to participate in — the kind where a founder describes the "journey" in polished, retrospective arcs, with all the ugly parts filed down. This post is not that. It is an honest answer to the question I get more than any other: *"What is it actually like to be building Vendoh alone, in Lagos, in 2026?"*

## The shape of a solo day

I wake up at 5:45am. Not because I am disciplined, but because the grid went down again at 4:12am and the inverter started beeping. I check two WhatsApp groups — the one with the payment processor, and the one with three vendors who have been beta-testing the app in Surulere. There is a bug report from one of them ("the voice doesn't understand 'generator'") and a question from the other ("when can I list my jollof package?"). Both are answered before I make coffee.

By 7:00am I am at my desk. The morning is the only time the house is quiet enough to do deep work, and deep work is what the product actually needs more of. I try to protect the morning block for coding or writing — anything that requires sustained thought. Everything else is triaged to the afternoon.

By 9:30am, a Lagos morning has reasserted itself. The diesel guy needs paying. The landlord has a "small" question. A meeting that was supposed to be at 10:00 has been moved to 11:30, and then to 2:00, and then to "tomorrow if that's okay?" I don't complain about this anymore. It is the weather.

## What solo actually means

When people hear "solo founder", they picture someone heroic and lonely, slogging through a to-do list alone. The reality is that "solo" doesn't mean "without help". It means "the final decision for every single thing lives in one head". Design, engineering, legal, finance, hiring, partnerships, vendor onboarding, the colour of the launch button, the exact wording of the refund policy, the choice of every underlying technology — every one of those is a decision I make and live with.

The upside is speed. There are no committees. There is no "let me loop in X before we decide". If something needs to ship, it ships. The downside is that every bad decision is my bad decision, and there is no one to diffuse the responsibility with.

I have learned, painfully, to tell the difference between the decisions that deserve an hour of thought and the decisions that deserve thirty seconds. Most of them deserve thirty seconds. The ones that deserve an hour are the ones that will be expensive to reverse: the commission rate, the verification tiers, the legal structure, the data model. Those I sit with. Everything else I ship and fix later.

## AI as a co-founder

The part that has changed the math of solo-building is AI. I do not mean "AI writes my code" — it doesn't, not in the way people outside the industry imagine. I mean that AI has collapsed the time it takes to do the research, review, and debugging loops that used to require a team. I can explore five architectural options before lunch. I can review my own PRs as if they were written by someone else. I can draft a legal clause, have it critiqued against three regulatory frameworks, and revise it, all before the landlord knocks.

Without this, Vendoh would require at least four more people today. With it, it requires one — with specific, expensive areas (legal review, security audit, regulatory compliance) escalated to real humans when the stakes warrant it. This is the shape of solo-building in 2026. The lone founder isn't really alone. They have a team of models at their desk.

## What I underestimated

Two things.

**The emotional load.** Building a product is a series of small hopes and small disappointments, and when there is nobody sitting next to you to share either, both get heavier than they should. I have had to get deliberate about calling a short list of friends, most of whom are not in tech, simply to hear a voice that is not speaking about Vendoh. That helps more than any productivity tool I have tried.

**The regulatory surface area.** Running a marketplace with escrow, identity verification, voice data processing, and a commission model means you are subject to the NDPA, the FCCPA, the Cybercrimes Act, the CBN, and a handful of other acronyms. None of that is difficult in isolation; it is the *combination*, and the need to keep them consistent across the Terms, the Privacy Policy and the Vendor Agreement, that is expensive. I learned this the hard way.

## What I would tell someone considering it

Build if you cannot not build. If the idea will haunt you until you ship it, you are going to ship it no matter what anyone tells you, and the only real question is whether the path is a straight line or a spiral. Go straight.

And if you're building in Lagos — specifically in Lagos — accept that the friction is part of the product. The product has to work despite the infrastructure. The product has to work despite the grid. The product has to work despite the traffic. Products designed in cities where the grid is never down cannot survive here, and that is a competitive advantage if you design into it instead of around it.

This is what it is really like. It is not glamorous. It is not always sustainable. But the mornings — the quiet mornings, when the code compiles and the voice orb pulses and a vendor in Surulere sends a voice note saying "this thing dey work" — the mornings make it worth it.
