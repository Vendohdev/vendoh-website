# Meet the Vendors: How Lagos Service Providers Are Building Businesses One Booking at a Time

**Published:** March 25, 2026
**Author:** Alex Nwoko
**Tag:** Vendor Stories
**Read Time:** 6 min read

---

Every time we tell the Vendoh story from the client side — "speak a query, get a verified vendor" — we only tell half the truth. The other half, and honestly the half that matters more, is what happens on the vendor side of the same transaction. The plumber in Ikeja who gets a booking notification at 9:14am and is standing in a client's kitchen by 10:30. The makeup artist in Yaba whose Saturdays are now booked three weeks in advance. The caterer in Surulere who no longer has to wake up wondering where next month's income is coming from.

These are the people Vendoh is built for. Three of them agreed to share their stories with us.

## Chioma — makeup artist, Yaba

Chioma started doing makeup for friends in 2018. By 2022, she had an Instagram account with decent engagement but an income that bounced between ₦40,000 and ₦180,000 a month depending on whether weddings were in season. Her biggest pain point wasn't talent — her work is genuinely excellent — it was predictability. Bookings came in through Instagram DMs that she had to read between other messages. Clients sometimes ghosted after agreeing a price. Sometimes she showed up and the venue had changed. Sometimes the client decided, at 11pm on Friday, that they'd rather have someone "more affordable".

What Chioma needed wasn't more visibility. She needed *structured* visibility: a system where a booking meant something, where payment was secured, and where her reputation compounded instead of being reset every time a conversation moved to a new Instagram DM thread. When we first explained the three-tier verification model and the structured booking flow to her, she asked a question we heard over and over again during vendor interviews: "So the price is agreed before I arrive?" Yes. "And the booking is on record if anything goes wrong?" Yes. She signed up on the spot.

## Emmanuel — AC and generator technician, Ikeja

Emmanuel has been fixing ACs and generators in the Ikeja axis for nine years. His customer base is real — around 70 regulars who call him directly — but his business is capped by his phone's call log. Every new customer has to be acquired through word-of-mouth. Every repeat customer has to remember his number. And every "can you come tomorrow?" is a verbal handshake that has no paper trail if something goes wrong.

When we asked him what "success" with a platform like Vendoh would look like, his answer was simple: *"Let me show up to jobs where I already know what's wrong and what it will cost, and let me not have to chase anybody for the money afterwards."* The voice-first search is what makes that possible. Clients describe the problem in their own words, which means Emmanuel can triage before he leaves the house. Upfront agreed pricing and in-app payment records are what close the loop on the second half.

## Tolu — private chef and caterer, Surulere

Tolu's story is the one that convinced us Vendoh needed to ship. She runs a small catering operation out of her home — mostly weekend owambe work, with some weekday corporate lunch contracts. Her problem isn't discovery (she's booked most weekends) — it's seasonality and collection. From January to March, bookings dry up. In December, she turns away work because she physically cannot cook for five weddings in one Saturday. And across the whole year, she estimates that roughly 15% of her invoices are paid late or not at all.

For Tolu, the voice-first angle matters less than dependable payments and the reputation layer. She wants every booking priced and confirmed before she starts, every completed job to add to her verified rating, and every rating to bring her closer to the "Certified" tier where she can charge premium rates without having to justify them. Vendoh's job is to be the infrastructure that makes all of that automatic.

## What we learned from 40+ vendor conversations

The pattern across every vendor interview we ran was the same: the problem is never skill, and it is rarely even discovery. The problem is that the current system — WhatsApp plus word-of-mouth plus bank transfers — does not accumulate trust. Every new client starts from zero. Every completed job disappears into the void. There is no compounding.

A marketplace is not a place where you go to find a vendor. A marketplace, properly built, is a place where every successful job makes the next successful job easier — for the vendor, for the client, and for the system as a whole. That compounding effect is what Nigeria's service economy has been missing, and it's what Vendoh exists to build.
